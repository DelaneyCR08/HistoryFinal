Artifact #1

Young Democrat Hat

These Young Democrats hate Maria Pepe. She was a twelve-year-old girl from New Jersey who became a symbol of the fight for equality in sports during the 1970s. In 1972, Pepe joined a Little League baseball team. League officials ordered her removal because she was a girl. Her experience became part of a larger legal and social battle over whether girls should have the same opportunities as boys in athletics. With support from the National Organization for Women (NOW), Pepe challenged Little League's policy. At the time, many people believed that girls shouldn’t participate in competitive sports with boys. They also believe that athletics were a male activity. These beliefs reflected those seen in education, careers, and public life. They limited opportunities for women because it was ‘for men.’ By standing up against discrimination, Pepe became a huge example of how young women challenged traditional gender roles. Her case ultimately helped lead to a 1974 ruling that required Little League Baseball to allow girls to participate. This opened opportunities for thousands of girls across the country and showed that athletic ability should not be determined by gender. Although Maria Pepe was only a child, her actions contributed to a large movement for equality.

Artifact #2

Title IX

Title IX was signed into law in 19722 as a part of the Education Amendments Act. This law is one of the most important pieces of legislation in the history of women's sports. The law prevents inequality and discrimination based on sex under any education program. That being said, Title IX was not written specifically for sports, but it drastically transformed opportunities for women in sports. Before Title IX, female athletes had significantly fewer opportunities than male athletes. Many schools offered limited sports programs for females, offered fewer scholarships, and provided inadequate facilities. Before Title IX, many females played a modified version of basketball known as six-on-six basketball. Under these rules, players were restricted to either offense or defense and would have to stay on that respective side of the court. They could not run the full length of the court. This was because people believed women weren't as capable as men to play five-on-five basketball. After Title IX was passed, this was changed; women could now play five-on-five basketball. Title IX represents a major victory in the fight for equality and shows how the women’s rights movement created opportunities that continue to benefit female athletes today. Without Title IX, many of the opportunities available to women in sports today would not exist.
