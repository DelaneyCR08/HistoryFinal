Feminism in Sports



How did women in sports represent the struggles for women's rights?





Feminism in Sports Outcome




The 1970s marked a huge turning point for women in sports. Women often faced discrimination, had limited opportunities, and faced many stereotypes that questioned their athletic abilities. Schools offered many fewer opportunities for female sports programs than they did for men's, and women were often excluded from leagues and competitions. These barriers reflected the larger societal beliefs that women should have fewer opportunities than men and that they can’t accomplish as much as men. As the feminist movement gained recognition and momentum in the 1960s and 1970s, women began challenging these restrictions on sports. Activists fought for equal rights in education, employment, and athletics. The passage of Title IX in 1972 and legal victories such as Maria Pepe’s Little League case helped open many doors for female athletes across the country. During the 1970s and 1980s, organizations such as the Association for Intercollegiate Athletics for Women (AIAW) worked to expand women’s college athletics. Traditional ideas about women in sports were increasingly challenged. Girls gained access to more teams, better equipment and facilities, and greater opportunities to compete. The impact of these changes can still be seen today. Women’s sports have experienced unprecedented growth in growth and popularity. Professional leagues such as the Women’s National Basketball Association (WNBA) have reached record attendance and TV ratings. They continue to expand their audiences and opportunities for athletes. Recently, WNBA players negotiated major salary increases, asking to be paid the same percentage of revenue as players in the NBA. The fight for equality is still happening today, but these women continue to advocate for equal treatment and professional respect. Women’s opportunities in sports continue to grow exponentially today. New leagues are being created left and right. New leagues such as Unrivaled, started in 2025, and Project B, scheduled to start late 2026, provide additional ways for athletes to earn salaries, get together, and work on their game professionally during the WNBA offseason. This helps combat a huge issue in many women's sports; there are often more talented players than roster spots. It’s not just basketball that continues to grow. Many sports are gaining new leagues. For example, the Women’s National Football Conference (WNFC) started in 2018 and includes the Jersey Shore Waves. Today, female athletes have access to opportunities that would’ve been unimaginable when Title IX was passed in 1972. College scholarships, professional Leagues, increased media coverage, and growing fan support demonstrate how far women's sports have come, and it will only continue to grow from here.
